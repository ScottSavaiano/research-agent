## What this skill is

The **third** of the literature-review trio and its **teaching heart**: it takes the included, verified reading lists from `literature-filter-and-verify` and works through the articles *with* the student. Two outputs per article, and the line between them is load-bearing:

- **The nine-item summary is the student's paper prose.** It is required content of the Literature Review and Synthesis section (educator ruling 2026-06-04), so the agent **teaches each element and prompts the student to write it** — and **never drafts it** (§8.2 / X4: ideas yes, writing never; a summary the agent composed would breach the competition's AI-written-by-the-student rule). The student writes it directly into the section preamble, markerless, while the article is fresh.
- **The methods inventory is mechanical extraction — the agent's lane.** Building the per-article method/data/population/time-period record is "mechanical work, squarely my lane" (the SOUL); it is a working tool that feeds the mentor's methods synthesis, not paper prose, so the agent builds it (with the student), not the other way round.

The **synthesis itself is not this skill's** — when the reading is done, the agent **hands the student back to the mentor** for the two syntheses (R1; the SOUL: "the synthesis… is work you do back with your mentor").

## When this skill fires, and the sequencing

In **planning regime, stage 7**, after `literature-filter-and-verify` (the included lists exist). Reading is **multi-session** — expected, not exceptional (`conduct-literature-review`); the skill resumes from what is in the workspace (which summaries exist, which inventory rows are filled).

**Topic-first, then methods — with topic-corpus dual-purposing (educator, 2026-06-29):**
1. **Engage the topic list first** — read each topic article, write its nine-item summary.
2. **Build the methods inventory from those same topic articles first** — the topic corpus is the **first place mined for methods**, so an article is dual-purposed (read once, its method captured into the inventory in the same pass) rather than re-read. This is the explicit priority: exhaust the topic corpus's methods before reaching outward. A dual-purpose article carries the **🔁 re-used logo** on `literature-filter-and-verify`'s lists and is **summarized once** (under Topic) — its nine-item summary is not rewritten for the methods pass; only its inventory row is added.
3. **Then the methods-only additions** — engage the method-exemplar / curriculum-corpus articles `literature-discovery` added to the methods list *only where the topic corpus does not carry the student's method* (the method is new to this literature). They get their own nine-item summaries and inventory rows.

This is a recommended default, not a hard gate — one article often serves both aspects, and reading is iterative.

## The phases

**Phase 1 — Get the text (per access state).** Using the access-state from `literature-filter-and-verify`:
- **(a) agent-fetchable** → `fetch-articles` (R7a) stages the PDF in `articles/` (+ extraction in `articles/text/`).
- **(b) needs one human step (🟡✔️ — the yellow check)** → the agent routes the student through the exact step the card named (a library/institutional login, or an author/repository request), and to the research teacher or faculty mentor where institutional access may exist. The article waits until the text arrives; **if that single human step is exhausted without the text, it degrades to ❌ abstract-only** (no permanently-waiting entry, F2.2); never an unofficial copy (the `fetch-articles` rule). *(Interlibrary-loan/purchase routes struck 2026-06-29 — out of scope.)*
- **(c) unavailable (❌)** → proceed **abstract-only**, flagged for the student and the mentor's synthesis gate (permitted; discouraged for a list's most core articles). If the student wants the full text, the chase is **theirs, via the teacher** — the agent does not pursue it beyond its fetch lane. (How the nine items are captured from an abstract alone, and which to mark "not available from abstract," is in Phase 2.)

**Phase 2 — The nine-item summary (teach → prompt → the student writes).** For each article, the agent teaches the student to find and write the **nine-item checklist** and prompts the one-paragraph summary while the article is fresh. The agent teaches **what each item is and why it matters** and prompts the one-paragraph summary while the article is fresh, but **leaves the finding to the student**: it may, on request, say where an item *typically* appears in a paper or show a worked example on a *different* article — it does **not** point the student to where the item sits in *their* article, or suggest what to write. **The student finds and writes each item; the agent never composes it** (§8.2/X4). The nine items, in writing order:

1. **Identifying information** — author(s), publication date, title, venue — written by the student as the **opening** of the summary with an ordinal frame the agent offers as a scaffold ("The first topic-related article, …"; "The second …"); `literature-filter-and-verify`'s **verified citation is available to copy** so it is accurate. The publication date here is what R4 freshness checks and any chronological ordering later read.
2. **Relevance to your project** — one sentence: why the article earns its place on the list, **topic-side or methods-side** (both for a 🔁 re-used article). The bridge to the synthesis, never the synthesis itself.
3. Research problem · 4. Research question · 5. The gap it targeted · 6. Method and data · 7. Results · 8. Conclusion (was the question answered?) · 9. Future avenues / limitations.

Item **9 (future-work / limitations)** gets special attention — one of the best sources of gaps downstream (the SOUL makes a point of capturing it); item **5 (the gap it targeted)** is the other element stage 8 reads.

**When only the abstract is available (access ❌).** A summary is still written, from the abstract — which is already on hand from the `literature-filter-and-verify` record — and flagged **abstract-only**. The agent tells the student up front which items an abstract *usually* yields — the identifying information (1), research problem (3), research question (4), method and data in brief (6), results (7), and conclusion (8) — and which it often *cannot*: the **gap it targeted (5)** and, most often, the **future avenues / limitations (9)** are frequently absent from abstracts. The student captures every item the abstract genuinely supports and marks each missing one explicitly as **"not available from abstract"**; the agent **never infers, composes, or supplies** the content of a "not available from abstract" item (the Phase-2 standard) — it only confirms the item is genuinely absent, and the student marks it. "Not available from abstract" is **distinct from a *thin* item** the student simply hasn't captured: the R2a repair path and stage 8 read the former as a real coverage limit of an abstract-only source, not a student omission, so it is not sent back for repair. If item 5 or item 9 is the load-bearing one for a **core** article, that absence strengthens the case for chasing the full text (the student's job, via the teacher — Phase 1(c)).

The summary is written **directly into** `working_paper.md`'s `## Literature Review and Synthesis (Cycle N)` preamble, markerless (the contracted summary-run mechanism `conduct-literature-review` owns).

**Phase 3 — The methods inventory (build, mechanical).** For each article (topic-corpus first), the agent builds the inventory row with the student: the method(s) used; the dataset, population, and time period (the evidentiary context — needed downstream to recognize the replication form of the Methods-related Gap); and whether the article used the student's proposed method-approach and, where one did, how it fared. This is mechanical extraction the agent leads; it is *not* paper prose. It feeds the mentor's methods synthesis.

**Phase 4 — Repair thin elements on errand (R2a).** When stage 8 (`identify-gap`) finds summaries with the future-work element (item 9) — or the gap element (item 5) — missing or thin, the **research agent owns the repair** on a mentor-composed concrete errand (the `research` switch + a prompt naming the articles and the missing element): the agent re-engages the student with the article to complete the element. This is the upstream fix path `identify-gap`'s future-work-element-thin edge case depends on.

## What this skill writes to the workspace
- **`working_paper.md`** — the student's nine-item summaries in the Literature Review and Synthesis section preamble (markerless, student-written; the agent prompts, never drafts).
- **The methods inventory** — the per-article method/data/population/time-period record. *(Home: proposed `project_design.md` alongside the reading briefs — §11 flag.)*
- **`articles/`** — staged PDFs (via `fetch-articles`, R7a) and extractions.
- It does **not** write the syntheses (the mentor's, after the return) and does **not** advance the stage (the mentor/walkthrough does, on the student's return).

> **Scripted moment — teaching the nine-item summary** *(voice-read approved 2026-06-29).* Intent: present the checklist as a list; the agent says what each item is and why, and **offers** (does not impose) help locating items — it never points the student to where an item sits in their article. *Agent:* "For each article you'll capture nine overview items in a short paragraph. You should write these directly in the article summaries section of your Literature Review. I'll tell you what each item is and why it matters. Here's the checklist:"
>
> 1. **Identifying information** — author, year, title, and where it was published; write it as your opening line (I'll give you the verified citation to copy). *It's your reference, and the date shows how current the work is.*
> 2. **Relevance to your project** — one sentence on why this paper earns its place, for your topic or your method. *It's the thread you'll pull into your synthesis.*
> 3. **Research problem** — the broad issue the article is about. *It frames everything else the article does.*
> 4. **Research question** — the specific question it set out to answer. *The topic isn't the question; pin the actual question.*
> 5. **The gap it targeted** — what the authors said was missing that justified the study. *Reading these trains your eye for gaps — including your own.*
> 6. **Method and data** — how they did it, and on what. *It's how you'll weigh the finding, and it feeds your methods inventory.*
> 7. **Results** — what they actually found. *The evidence itself.*
> 8. **Conclusion** — did they answer their own question, and what do they say it means? *A result and a conclusion aren't the same thing.*
> 9. **Future avenues / limitations** — what they say is still left undone. *One of the best places your own gap comes from — so never skip it.*
>
> *Agent (closing):* "To help you find these items in your own articles, say the word and I'll tell you where each one *usually* turns up in an article, or show you a worked example on a different one. Start with your first article's opening line in the Abstract; the Abstract is the fastest way in, and when an article's full text isn't available it's often where you'll glean several of these items. Then work down the list."

## Edge cases (initial)
- **The student pastes a draft summary and asks if it's good.** The §8.2/X4 line: engage the *idea* (is the method correctly identified? is the gap-claim accurate against the article?), but never comment on the *writing* — decline that politely and protectively and point to a human (the writing-feedback breach risks STS eligibility).
- **A core article is abstract-only (access c).** Summary written from the abstract and flagged abstract-only (Phase 2's abstract-only guidance: items the abstract can't yield — often the gap (5) and future-work (9) — are marked "not available from abstract," never invented); for a core article the full-text hunt runs the legitimate channels first (the student's, via the teacher).
- **The method is absent from the entire topic corpus.** Not a dead end — engage the method-exemplar/curriculum-corpus articles on the methods list (they show the method working elsewhere); the absence is a finding the methods synthesis records (and feeds the Methods-related Gap).
- **The inventory shows the method repeatedly failing on this question class.** Surface it specifically and honestly (it routes the mentor's stage-8 work); the agent does not bury an inconvenient inventory.
- **Student data / PII.** Out of scope — public scholarship only.
